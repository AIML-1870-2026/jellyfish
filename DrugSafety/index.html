How to read this data

This tool queries the OpenFDA API to surface publicly available drug safety information from three FDA databases. It is built for educational exploration — not clinical decision-making.

Three data sources used:
• Label data — official FDA-approved prescribing information, reviewed before a drug is approved.
• Adverse event reports (FAERS) — voluntary self-reports submitted after a drug is on the market.
• Enforcement records — drug recalls and market withdrawals with severity classifications.

Each data source tells a different story. Label warnings reflect what was known at approval. Adverse event data reflects real-world experience. Recall history reflects post-market safety actions.

Always consult a healthcare professional before making any medication decisions.

The FDA's Adverse Event Reporting System (FAERS) collects voluntary reports of side effects submitted by patients, doctors, pharmacists, and manufacturers.

Key limitations:
• Reports are voluntary — many adverse events are never reported.
• Correlation ≠ causation. A report doesn't mean the drug caused the reaction.
• Higher counts ≠ more dangerous. Popular drugs naturally accumulate more reports.
• The same reaction may be reported multiple times for one patient.

Use adverse event data as a signal for what types of reactions have been observed — not as a definitive measure of risk.

Label data comes from the FDA-approved prescribing information — the "package insert." This is reviewed and approved by the FDA before a drug can be marketed.

Label warnings include:
• Boxed warnings — the most serious, required when there's risk of life-threatening adverse reactions.
• Contraindications — situations where the drug should never be used.
• Warnings & Precautions — important risks requiring monitoring.
• Adverse reactions — side effects observed in clinical trials.

Unlike FAERS reports, label data reflects clinically verified information. Labels are updated over time as new safety information emerges.

The FDA classifies drug recalls into three severity levels:

  • CLASS I — Most serious. Reasonable probability of serious health consequences or death. Example: contaminated injectable drugs or dangerous labeling errors.
  • CLASS II — Moderate. May cause temporary or reversible health consequences. Remote probability of serious harm.
  • CLASS III — Least serious. Unlikely to cause adverse health consequences. Often minor labeling or packaging issues.

A recall doesn't necessarily mean the drug is unsafe — it may relate to a specific batch. Consult your pharmacist if you have a recalled medication.

The number of adverse event reports is heavily influenced by factors unrelated to how dangerous a drug actually is.

  • Prescription volume: Drugs taken by millions naturally accumulate far more reports than rare specialty drugs.
  • Media attention: When a drug receives news coverage, reporting spikes — even if the underlying risk hasn't changed.
  • Reporting culture: Patients and doctors vary widely in how often they file FAERS reports.
  • Drug age: Older drugs have had more time to accumulate reports than newer ones.
  • Manufacturer reporting: Drug companies must report serious adverse events, creating spikes for widely prescribed drugs.

For a fairer comparison you'd normalize by prescriptions dispensed — data not available in the public FAERS dataset.

Some drug combinations carry well-documented risks that appear in official label warnings but aren't captured in a single drug's FAERS data.

Classic high-risk combinations:
• Warfarin + NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) — significantly increased bleeding risk, including life-threatening internal bleeding.
• MAO inhibitors + SSRIs / SNRIs / tramadol — can cause serotonin syndrome, a potentially fatal condition.
• Methotrexate + NSAIDs — can raise methotrexate toxicity to dangerous levels.
• ACE inhibitors + potassium-sparing diuretics — risk of dangerous hyperkalemia.
• Statins + certain antibiotics / antifungals — increased risk of rhabdomyolysis.

This tool does not check for drug interactions. Always review potential interactions with your pharmacist or doctor.

How to interpret adverse event data

The FDA's Adverse Event Reporting System (FAERS) collects voluntary reports of side effects submitted by patients, doctors, pharmacists, and manufacturers.

Key limitations to keep in mind:
• Reports are voluntary — many adverse events are never reported.
• Correlation ≠ causation. A report doesn't mean the drug caused the reaction.
• Higher counts ≠ more dangerous. Popular drugs naturally accumulate more reports because more people take them.
• The same reaction may be reported multiple times for a single patient.

A drug with 500,000 adverse event reports may be safer than one with 500 reports, simply because far more people are taking it. Use this data as a signal for what types of reactions have been observed — not a definitive measure of risk.

Understanding recall classifications

The FDA classifies drug recalls into three severity levels based on the health risk posed:

  • CLASS I — Most serious. Reasonable probability of serious health consequences or death.
  • CLASS II — Moderate. May cause temporary or reversible health consequences, or remote probability of serious harm.
  • CLASS III — Least serious. Unlikely to cause adverse health consequences. Often minor labeling or packaging issues.

A recall doesn't necessarily mean the drug is unsafe — it may relate to a specific batch. Consult your pharmacist or doctor if you have a recalled medication.
